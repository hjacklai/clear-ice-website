THE 72-HOUR PROCESS

How we make
clear ice.

The reason clear ice looks the way it does is not magic and not a finishing trick. It is a slow, controlled freezing process that takes 72 hours from the moment the water enters the vessel to the moment the cube reaches your glass. Here is what actually happens.

Step 1. Purified water.

Tap water carries calcium, magnesium, chlorine, fluoride, and absorbed gases. Every one of those leaves a trace in the final ice, both visually (cloudy core) and flavour-wise (the slight chemical edge of melting tap-water ice).

We start with multi-stage filtered water. Minerals are stripped down. Dissolved gases are released. What is left is the closest thing to neutral, pure H2O that you can get without lab equipment.

Step 2. Directional freezing.

Here is the key principle. If you freeze water from all sides at once, the freezing front converges in the middle. Air bubbles, minerals and impurities have nowhere to go, so they get trapped in the centre. That is your cloudy core.

Directional freezing insulates every side of the vessel except the top. The cold attacks from one direction only. As the water freezes from the surface downward, air and impurities are pushed ahead of the freezing front, ending up at the very bottom of the block. The top 80% of the column ends up optically clear. The cloudy bottom 20% is what gets scored off later.

This is the same principle that lets glaciers form crystal-clear ice in nature. We just speed it up to 72 hours and run it on a schedule.

Step 3. The slow part.

Speed is the enemy. If you push the temperature too low too fast, ice freezes with internal stress fractures. Visually it looks fine, until light hits it at the wrong angle and you see hairline cracks running through the cube. Structurally it is weaker, so it melts unevenly. Drinks-wise, those fractures act as surface area, accelerating dilution.

We freeze slowly. 72 hours from start to finish. The block holds its clarity, its strength, and its slow-melt behaviour.

Step 4. Cut and polish.

The big block comes out of the freezer. The cloudy bottom strip gets scored and removed. The remaining clear column gets sectioned: into 5 cm cubes, 5.5 cm spheres, 4 by 11 cm cylinders, 4 by 4 by 10 cm columns, depending on what is being filled for the day.

Each piece is then hand-polished to remove any frost or surface haze from the cutting process. The final cube is glass-clear.

Step 5. Insulated delivery.

A clear cube melts the second it hits a warm air. Standard insulated boxes give us about two hours of window before melt becomes visible. Our delivery setup uses vacuum-insulated containers that extend that window to four-plus hours. We pack the ice, seal the container, ship it, and you crack it open at your venue with no visible melt.

Why home freezer "clear ice" mostly fails.

  • Temperature cycling. Home freezers swing between -18 and -10 Celsius as the compressor cycles. Each swing introduces micro-fractures. Industrial freezers hold a stable temperature.
  • Food odours. Ice absorbs aromas. Anything strong in your freezer ends up in the cube. A clean freeze chamber is dedicated to ice only.
  • No directional control. The cooler-method trick (cooler with the lid off in your freezer) does work partially, but you only get a small clear block and you need to commit your freezer to a 24-hour single-purpose job.
  • Cutting is hard. A clear block is harder than freezer ice. Cutting it to a clean 5 cm cube needs a heated knife, a serrated blade and steady hands. Most home attempts end in cracked, asymmetric cubes.

If you enjoy the DIY route, great, it is a fun project. If you just want bar-grade clear ice without the setup, that is what we are for.
