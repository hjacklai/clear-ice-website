THE CRAFT

How clear ice
is made.

You do not need to know every secret of the trade to appreciate clear ice. But it helps to understand why this stuff costs more than a bag of supermarket cubes, why it lasts so much longer in your glass, and what to look for when you buy.

The principle: slow, one-direction freezing.

When water freezes from all sides at the same time, air bubbles and dissolved minerals get trapped in the middle as the freezing fronts meet. That is the cloudy core you see in your ice tray.

Clear ice is made by freezing in one direction only. The water freezes gradually from the top down. As the ice front advances, it pushes air and impurities ahead of it. The top of the block ends up optically pure. The cloudy fraction at the bottom is trimmed away.

This is a well-known principle in food science. The hard part is doing it consistently, at production scale, every day, with cubes that come out the same size and clarity every time.

It takes patience.

Around 72 hours per block. Freezing too fast introduces internal stress fractures: hairline cracks that look fine until light hits them at an angle, and that weaken the cube structurally so it melts unevenly in your glass. Slow is non-negotiable.

Water matters.

Tap water carries minerals, chlorine, fluoride and absorbed gases. Even the best directional freeze will not fully fix bad water. Clear ice starts with multi-stage filtered, purified water before anything else.

Then the human part.

A finished clear ice block is then cut and shaped into the formats people actually drink with: 5 cm cubes, 5.5 cm spheres, 4 by 11 cm cylinders, 4 by 4 by 10 cm columns. This step is where craft shows up. Cleanly cut, square-edged cubes signal a supplier that cares. Asymmetric, chipped or frost-coated cubes signal the opposite.

Why home methods rarely work.

  • Your freezer cycles temperature. Compressors switch on and off, swinging temperature by several degrees. Each swing introduces micro-fractures into the ice.
  • Your freezer holds food. Ice absorbs aromas. Anything strong in there ends up in the cube. Hard to avoid in a home freezer.
  • The cooler trick is small-batch and slow. The popular cooler-method gives you one partially clear block per 24-hour cycle, ties up your freezer, and still needs hand cutting.
  • Cutting clear ice is harder than it looks. The block is denser than freezer ice. A clean 2 inch cube needs a serrated blade, a heated knife, steady hands and tolerance for waste.

DIY is a fun project. For service or guests, buying professionally produced clear ice is faster, more consistent and usually cheaper than the equipment and time you would spend trying.

What to look for when you buy.

  • Clarity all the way through. Hold the cube up to a light. No white core, no haze, no hairline cracks.
  • No smell when it melts. A drop of meltwater on your tongue should taste like nothing.
  • No cracking under pour. Pour a chilled spirit over the cube. A well-made block will not split.
  • Crisp edges. Hand-cut shapes with square, sharp corners signal craft. Frost or rounded edges signal rushed work.
  • Packaging arrives dry. Insulated, sealed delivery means the ice did not start melting in transit.

FAQ

Why is clear ice clear and regular ice cloudy?+

Regular ice freezes from all sides at once, trapping air bubbles, dissolved minerals and absorbed odours in the centre as a cloudy core. Clear ice is made using directional freezing, so impurities are pushed out as the ice forms instead of trapped inside.

How long does it take to make clear ice?+

Around 72 hours per block under controlled conditions. Slow freezing matters: too fast and the block develops internal stress fractures that look bad and weaken the cube.

Can I make clear ice at home?+

Sort of, with the cooler-in-the-freezer trick. Most attempts run into temperature cycling, food-odour absorption and difficulty cutting a clean cube without specialised tools. Results are inconsistent. Most people buy professional clear ice instead.

What should I look for when buying clear ice?+

Optical clarity all the way through (no white core), no off-smells when it melts, no cracking when liquid is poured over it, and packaging that arrives dry and melt-free. Hand-finished cubes with crisp edges signal that the supplier cares about the craft.
